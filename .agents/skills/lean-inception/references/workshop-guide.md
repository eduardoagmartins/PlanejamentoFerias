## Purpose

Lean Inception is a collaborative workshop, created by Paulo Caroli, to align a group around the product direction and define the MVP. It is intentionally lean: remove activities that do not directly help define the product and its MVP, and keep architecture, detailed backlog writing, and delivery planning for later.

Use it to help a team:

- Share a product vision.
- Clarify what the product is, is not, does, and does not do.
- Agree on business objectives and trade-offs.
- Understand personas and important journeys.
- Brainstorm features from objectives, personas, and journeys.
- Evaluate features by confidence, effort, business value, and user value.
- Sequence features into delivery waves.
- Identify the MVP and near-term increments.
- Define expected outcomes, metrics, risks, cost, and schedule.

## Fit and non-fit

Lean Inception fits well when:

- A team needs to define an MVP after discovery, experiments, ideation, or business strategy work.
- A large initiative needs to start lean, deliver value earlier, and validate valuable features with users.
- A startup or smaller organization has tested pre-software MVPs and now needs to shape a software product.
- The initiative has high innovation and high business impact, requiring strong cross-functional alignment.
- The team is moving from discovery toward delivery in dual-track product development.
- The team is at the Define stage of the Double Diamond.
- A Business Model Canvas or strategy exists and the team needs to define the MVP Canvas and first concrete step.
- The output will feed Spec Kit or another spec-driven process.

Do not position Lean Inception as the main method when the user really needs:

- Discovery or user research to generate initial learning.
- Design Sprint to decide or validate a prototype.
- PI Planning to align many teams around a program.
- WSJF, RICE, or similar techniques to prioritize a portfolio.
- Technical workshops to map solution details.
- Retrospective or team process activities to define ways of working.
- Product Backlog Building to plan sprint-level user stories.
- User Story Mapping to map detailed user journeys into stories.

## MVP principles

Use these principles to guide every synthesis:

- The MVP exists to validate a small set of business and product hypotheses.
- The MVP should be a thin slice of the future product, not a disconnected technical layer.
- The slice must be valuable for the business, usable for users, feasible for the team, and remarkable enough to generate real feedback.
- "Viable" matters: avoid experiments that create a damaging first impression or put users into a broken experience.
- Validate and learn quickly; do not use "fail fast" as permission to harm users or the brand.
- A feature belongs in the MVP only when it is tied to a target persona, a journey, a desired outcome, and a validation metric.
- Future increments should remain provisional. Create MVP Canvas detail for the first MVP, sometimes the next two, but expect learning to change later increments.

## Participants

- Active members: product, business, design/UX, engineering, and people with knowledge of users and operations. They participate throughout the workshop.
- Stakeholders: sponsors, final users, legal, sales, marketing, operations, or other interested departments. They usually join the kick-off and the showcase.
- Facilitator: person responsible for guiding the process, protecting participation quality, managing timeboxes, and helping the group converge.

## Preparation

Before the workshop:

- Clarify the business reason, product/problem area, expected decision, and boundaries.
- Invite the multidisciplinary team and confirm stakeholder moments.
- Choose an experienced facilitator.
- Prepare the room or remote board and keep the same working space throughout the workshop when possible.
- Collect prior discovery, experiments, market research, strategy artifacts, constraints, and assumptions.
- Prepare templates for each activity and a visible parking lot for unresolved questions.
- Prepare materials: board or canvas, sticky notes, markers, voting dots or equivalent, timer, and shared repository for final notes.
- Make explicit how the output will be used after the workshop, especially if the next step is Spec Kit.

## Facilitation practices

The facilitator leads the process, not the product decision. Use a higher verbal presence when explaining activities, then step back during decisions so the team owns the outcome.

Use a parking lot for off-topic but valuable conversations. Introduce it early, move deferred topics visibly, and revisit it at planned checkpoints.

Use an agenda burn-up or equivalent visible agenda tracker. Show current time, planned activities, completed activities, and drift. When the workshop is behind, adjust early by narrowing discussion, using the parking lot, shortening a low-risk activity, or splitting a group.

Use icebreakers only when they improve collaboration. Keep them short and relevant to the group's energy.

Capture decisions as they happen. The most useful outputs are often the notes behind feature cards: assumptions, disagreements, risks, and why a choice was made.

## One-week agenda template

Adapt this agenda to context. A common shape is one week with half-day sessions.

### Monday

Morning: Kick-off, framing, and product vision.

- Objective: align on why the workshop exists and what product direction is being explored.
- Output: shared product vision and workshop expectations.

Afternoon: Product Is / Is not / Does / Does not; clarify objectives.

- Objective: clarify product boundaries and business focus.
- Output: consensus on scope boundaries and prioritized product objectives.

### Tuesday

Morning: Trade-offs and personas.

- Objective: expose decision criteria and user/customer profiles.
- Output: explicit trade-off ranking and prioritized personas or segments.

Afternoon: User journeys.

- Objective: map steps each persona follows to achieve a goal and where the product may help.
- Output: journey maps with pain points, opportunities, and touchpoints.

### Wednesday

Morning: Feature brainstorming.

- Objective: generate candidate features that support personas, objectives, and journeys.
- Output: feature inventory written as user/product interactions.

Afternoon: Technical, UX, and business review.

- Objective: evaluate features by feasibility, confidence, user value, business value, risks, dependencies, and effort.
- Output: annotated feature set and clearer trade-offs.

### Thursday

Morning: Features in user journeys.

- Objective: connect features to journey moments and remove orphan or low-value ideas.
- Output: journey-feature map.

Afternoon: Sequencer.

- Objective: create an incremental delivery sequence consistent with MVP thinking.
- Output: ordered waves, dependencies, and MVP candidate boundary.

### Friday

Morning: MVP Canvas and cost/schedule conversation.

- Objective: define the MVP proposal, expected result, metrics, selected features, personas, journeys, cost, schedule, and validation strategy.
- Output: MVP Canvas and confidence/risk view.

Afternoon: Showcase and handoff.

- Objective: share the proposed solution and MVP with stakeholders, gather feedback, simplify where needed, and decide whether to proceed.
- Output: decisions, feedback, next actions, open questions, and Spec Kit-ready discovery handoff when applicable.

## Activity playbook

### Product vision

Use the classic product vision template as a collaborative sentence-builder. Capture target customer, problem, product name/category, key benefit, alternatives, and differentiator. Expect the first version to be awkward; refine it as a group until it is coherent.

### Is / Is not / Does / Does not

Create four quadrants:

- Is: nouns and adjectives that describe the product.
- Is not: explicit boundaries or identities to avoid.
- Does: actions the product enables.
- Does not: actions the product should not do, at least for the MVP.

Use this to clarify strategy, non-goals, and future-but-not-now items.

### Clarify objectives

Ask each participant to write three business objectives for the product. Cluster similar objectives, discard items that are not true objectives, and rewrite a short shared list. These objectives become prioritization criteria and later success criteria.

### Understand trade-offs

List relevant product qualities such as security, usability, scalability, speed, compliance, cost, or operational simplicity. Force relative ranking so not everything can be "most important." Use the result to resolve later conflicts and to guide Spec Kit constraints.

### Personas

For each persona capture role/segment, goals, needs, pains, context of use, and why this persona matters for the MVP. Use empathy maps when the group needs deeper user understanding. Treat personas as living hypotheses, not final truth.

### User journeys

Map the steps a persona takes to achieve a goal. Keep the level of detail balanced: enough to reveal product opportunities, not so detailed that the group starts doing screen design or story mapping. Use alternate journeys only when they change the MVP decision.

### Feature brainstorming

Start from prioritized objectives and personas, then ask what the product must enable. Write each feature as a simple user or product capability. Limit personas/objectives when the matrix grows too large.

Use an investment-style prioritization activity when needed: give groups a small budget and ask where they would invest to learn or create the most value.

Discard or challenge features that do not connect to any persona, objective, or journey.

### Technical, UX, and business review

Review each feature through two steps:

- Confidence: discuss how well the team understands what to build and how to build it. Mark low, medium, or high confidence.
- Value and effort: mark effort, business value, and UX/user value on a small comparative scale.

Capture notes behind each feature card: assumptions, disagreements, dependencies, and uncertainties. These notes are often the seed for Spec Kit research and risks.

### Features in journeys

Read each journey slowly and map feature cards to the journey steps they support or improve. Add missing features when a journey exposes a gap. Challenge features that map to no important journey.

### Showcase

Show the MVP proposal, not every workshop artifact. Include the product vision, target personas, prioritized journeys, MVP boundary, selected features, expected result, metrics, major risks, and decisions needed.

## Sequencer guidance

The sequencer orders feature cards into delivery waves. Use it to decide the MVP boundary and future increments.

Work from the most important persona and journey. Ask relative priority questions such as "which of these two matters more for MVP validation?" rather than asking whether each feature is important.

Use simple wave rules:

- Limit work in progress by capping each wave at a small number of feature cards.
- Avoid waves dominated by low-confidence work.
- Keep effort per wave balanced.
- Keep business and user value high in every early wave.
- Place dependencies before the features that depend on them.
- Use the first point where a minimal viable validation exists as the MVP boundary.

If using Caroli's common numeric rules, a wave can hold up to three feature cards, should avoid too much red/yellow confidence, should not exceed about five effort marks, should include enough business and user value marks, and must respect dependencies.

## Effort, time, and cost

Use rough estimation only after the team has a sequencer. The goal is not perfect delivery forecasting; the goal is a credible sense of MVP investment.

Recommended approach:

- Select two or three representative waves.
- Break selected feature cards into smaller tasks or user-story-like chunks.
- Size tasks relatively with simple sizes such as P/M/G or XS/S/M/L.
- Estimate how long one person or one pair would take for representative tasks in each size.
- Use the average wave effort to discuss cost and schedule.
- Include non-development costs such as marketing, operations, infrastructure, compliance, content, training, or dependencies.

Keep this as an input to planning. Do not let estimation take over the discovery workshop.

## MVP Canvas

Use the MVP Canvas as the final synthesis. Fill it after vision, objectives, personas, journeys, features, review, and sequencing are visible.

Capture seven blocks:

- MVP proposal: what this MVP is meant to validate.
- Segmented personas: who this MVP is for and whether it can be released to a smaller group.
- Journeys: which journeys are served or improved.
- Features: what will be built and which actions are simplified or improved.
- Expected result: what learning or outcome is expected.
- Metrics: how the team will validate the hypothesis.
- Cost and schedule: investment, timing, dependencies, and external work.

Use the Canvas to simplify:

- If the proposal is broad, narrow it.
- If personas are broad, segment them.
- If too many journeys are included, pick the ones that prove the MVP.
- If features are not minimal or viable, adjust them.
- If metrics are missing, do not proceed to implementation-facing specs yet.
- If the cost/schedule discussion reveals a large gap, revisit scope.

## Remote facilitation

For remote Lean Inception:

- Use a shared board with one area per activity.
- Keep instructions visible inside each activity area.
- Use shorter facilitation blocks and explicit breaks.
- Decide how voting, grouping, and comments will work before the session.
- Keep a visible decision log and parking lot.
- Assign a co-facilitator when the group is large.
- Take board snapshots before moving cards between templates.

## Deliverable formats

### Agenda

Include day/session, timebox, objective, participants, activity flow, inputs, outputs, and facilitator notes.

### Facilitation guide

Include script, board setup, prompts, timeboxes, decision points, materials, and expected artifacts.

### Synthesis report

Include context, participants, major decisions, product vision, objectives, trade-offs, personas, journeys, selected features, sequencer summary, MVP Canvas summary, metrics, risks, open questions, and next steps.

### Decision memo

Include the MVP recommendation, evidence considered, alternatives rejected, assumptions, risks, required investment, and near-term plan.
